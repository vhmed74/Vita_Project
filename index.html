Smart Environmental Monitoring System
For Neonatal Incubator

A comprehensive IoT-based upgrade kit designed to enhance conventional incubators with real-time monitoring, automated control, and remote access capabilities.

Faculty of Applied Health Sciences Technology

Menoufia University - Technology of Biomedical Equipment

Abstract

System Overview

Premature infants require a carefully controlled environment to maintain physiological stability. This project introduces a smart monitoring and control system for neonatal incubators.

It utilizes multiple sensors to measure temperature, humidity, air quality, sound levels, and infant weight in real time. A central microcontroller processes the data to regulate the environment and trigger alarms when necessary.

IoT integration allows healthcare professionals to monitor the neonate remotely via a mobile app, ensuring a rapid response to any critical changes.

Clinical Importance

Why Neonatal Incubators Matter

  • Thermoregulation: Prevents hypothermia by maintaining a neutral thermal environment.
  • Respiratory Support: Delivers controlled oxygen and CPAP therapy.
  • Infection Control: Provides a sealed physical barrier against airborne pathogens.
  • Fluid Homeostasis: High humidity prevents transepidermal water loss.
  • Continuous Observation: Real-time monitoring of vital signs.

Problem Statement

The Need for an Upgrade

Traditional neonatal incubators lack integrated smart monitoring capabilities, requiring constant manual supervision by nursing staff.

While commercial "Smart Incubators" exist, they are extremely expensive, placing a heavy financial burden on developing healthcare facilities.

Our project bridges this gap by creating a highly capable, low-cost upgrade kit that retrofits existing traditional incubators into intelligent, IoT-enabled life-support systems.

Project Objectives

Key Goals of the System

Environmental Control

Continuously monitor and automatically regulate incubator air temperature and humidity.

Physiological Tracking

Monitor the infant's core body temperature and precise weight changes continuously.

Hazard Detection

Assess air quality (MQ135) and environmental noise levels (LM386) to prevent neonatal stress.

IoT Connectivity

Transmit all sensor readings wirelessly to a dedicated mobile application for remote tracking.

The Proposed Solution

A Smart Upgrade Kit

We developed a modular, plug-and-play electronics suite that attaches to conventional incubators.

  • Cost-Effective: Avoids the need to purchase entirely new incubator units.
  • Comprehensive: Merges 5 different sensing modalities into one dashboard.
  • Fail-Safe: Includes local LCD and buzzer alarms that function even if Wi-Fi drops.

System Architecture

4-Layer Design Philosophy

  • 1. Sensing Layer: DHT22, DS18B20, MQ135, LM386, and Load Cell gather raw data.
  • 2. Processing Layer: Arduino UNO acts as the core, executing logic.
  • 3. Actuation Layer: SSR controls AC heating; Fans manage humidity.
  • 4. Interface Layer: LCD, Alarms, and ESP32 Wi-Fi gateway to the app.

Hardware Overview

Core Modules

  • Microcontrollers: Arduino UNO & ESP32-CAM.
  • Sensors: DHT22, DS18B20, MQ135, LM386, Load cell with HX711 Amplifier.
  • Actuators: Solid-State Relay (SSR), DC Ventilation Fan.
  • Interface: 16x4 I2C LCD Display, 5V Buzzer, LED Indicators, Push Button.

Hardware Specifications

Processing: Arduino UNO & ESP32

Dual-Processor Architecture

Arduino UNO (ATmega328P): Chosen for its robust 5V logic which interfaces perfectly with relays and analog sensors. It operates at 16MHz and handles life-critical sensor polling and actuator control continuously, ensuring the core medical functions never freeze.

Arduino Specs: 5V Operating Voltage | 14 Digital I/O | 6 Analog Inputs

ESP32: Acts as a dedicated high-speed communication gateway. Operating at 240MHz with built-in Wi-Fi, it offloads all network tasks from the Arduino, preventing network latency from delaying the heating control loop.

ESP32 Specs: 3.3V Logic | Built-in Wi-Fi 802.11 b/g/n | Dual-Core Tensilica LX6

Hardware Specifications

Environmental: DHT22 & DS18B20

Precision Thermoregulation

DHT22 (Ambient Temp & Hum): Provides pre-calibrated digital outputs. It offers exceptional long-term stability and accuracy compared to the cheaper DHT11, which is crucial for preventing neonatal hypothermia.

DHT22 Specs: Temp Range: -40 to 80°C (±0.5°C) | Hum Range: 0-100% (±2%)

DS18B20 (Infant Body Temp): A digital thermometer utilizing the 1-Wire bus. Because it sends data digitally, it is completely immune to electrical noise from AC heaters and motors, ensuring perfect body temperature readings.

DS18B20 Specs: Temp Range: -55°C to +125°C | Accuracy: ±0.5°C | Waterproof

Hardware Specifications

Measurement: HX711 & Load Cell

Micro-Weight Tracking

Aluminum Load Cell (20kg): Placed securely under the incubator mattress. It converts physical pressure into an electrical resistance change (strain gauge) to track the neonate's growth.

HX711 Amplifier: The resistance change from the load cell is in millivolts, far too small for the Arduino to read. The HX711 is a precision 24-bit Analog-to-Digital Converter designed specifically for weigh scales, allowing us to detect weight fluctuations down to the gram without removing the infant from the incubator.

HX711 Specs: 24-bit high precision ADC | Selectable Gain (32, 64, 128) | I2C-like interface

Hardware Specifications

Hazard Detection: MQ135 & LM386

Holistic Hazard Detection

MQ135 (Air Quality): Standard incubators monitor oxygen, but the MQ135 detects harmful gases like ammonia, CO2, benzene, and alcohol vapors. This protects the infant's respiratory system from airborne cleaning chemicals.

MQ135 Specs: Target gases: NH3, NOx, Alcohol, CO2 | Analog output proportional to gas concentration

LM386 (Sound Sensor): The rapidly developing cochlear pathways of a neonate are sensitive to noise (>45dB causes stress). This sensor features an adjustable gain amplifier acting as an acoustic sentinel to alert staff of excessive ambient noise.

Hardware Specifications

Actuation: Solid-State Relay (SSR)

Silent & Safe AC Switching

To control the high-voltage AC heater, we explicitly selected a Solid-State Relay (SSR) instead of a traditional mechanical electromagnetic relay.

Clinical Benefit: Mechanical relays produce a loud "clicking" sound when switching, inducing acoustic stress in neonates. SSRs use internal opto-couplers to switch loads completely silently. Furthermore, SSRs generate zero electrical arcing (sparking) inside the oxygen-rich incubator environment, eliminating fire hazards.

SSR Specs: Input: 3-32V DC | Output: 24-380V AC | Zero-Cross Switching | Complete Opto-Isolation

Software Architecture

Embedded Logic

  • Environment: Developed entirely in Arduino IDE using C/C++.
  • Libraries: `DHT.h`, `OneWire.h`, `DallasTemperature.h`, `HX711.h`, and `LiquidCrystal_I2C.h` streamline hardware interfacing.
  • Control Loop: The main `loop()` continuously polls sensors without using blocking delays (`millis()` timing), ensuring instant alarm response.
  • Calibration: Sensor offsets (e.g., subtracting 500g for mattress weight) are handled directly in software.

Alarm & Notification System

Fail-Safe Emergency Response

Threshold Logic

Temp < 26°C or > 35°C. Humidity < 50% or > 60%. Noise > 600. Air Quality > 500 PPM.

Local Indicators

A flashing Red LED and a piercing 1500Hz Buzzer tone activate instantly upon fault detection.

LCD Diagnosis

The 16x4 I2C LCD dynamically overwrites the screen to show the exact source of the error (e.g., "!! DANGER: High Temp !!").

Mute Protocol

A physical hardware push-button allows nurses to silence the buzzer, but the Red LED remains ON until the physical fault is fully cleared.

IoT Integration

The Neo Care App

We decouple the critical life-support logic from the internet using UART communication. The Arduino sends a string of sensor values to the ESP32.

  • Wi-Fi Connectivity: ESP32 connects to the hospital's local network using TCP/IP protocol.
  • Web Service: Data is pushed via HTTP to a cloud database.
  • Mobile Application: Doctors can view live temperature graphs, weight logs, and receive instant push notifications if an alarm triggers, from anywhere in the world.

Testing & Validation

Ensuring Reliability

  • Sensor Calibration: The MQ135 was pre-heated for 24 hours and base-lined in fresh air (180 reading). The Load Cell was calibrated using standard known weights (2.5kg, 3.0kg).
  • Unit Testing: Each of the hardware subsystems was tested individually before final integration.
  • Stress Testing: Intentional fault injection (e.g., heating the DS18B20 manually, introducing noise) successfully triggered the alarms in under 2 seconds.

Experimental Results

System Performance

Thermal Stability

The SSR effectively cycled the heater ON at < 26°C and OFF > 35°C, maintaining the desired thermal envelope without fail.

Weight Accuracy

The HX711 and Load cell achieved an impressive error margin of just ±0.01 kg (10 grams) across multiple tests.

Communication Latency

Average response time from physical fault detection to the ESP32 firing a mobile notification was measured at ~1-2 seconds.

Body Temp Precision

DS18B20 digital readings perfectly matched clinical reference thermometers with a maximum deviation of 0.1°C.

Future Recommendations

Additional Biomedical Monitoring

  • Heart Rate Monitoring: Continuously measure pulse rate in real time for early detection of cardiac abnormalities such as bradycardia or tachycardia.
  • Respiratory Rate Monitoring: Continuously measure breathing rate to facilitate early detection of respiratory distress, apnea episodes, or irregular breathing patterns.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Incorporate an ECG module to monitor electrical heart activity, providing valuable diagnostic information regarding cardiac function.
  • Pulse Oximetry (SpO2): Integrate an oxygen saturation sensor to monitor blood oxygen levels, significantly improving the overall physiological assessment of the newborn.
